  • Albert Einstein (1879–1955) was a German-born theoretical physicist who is widely regarded as one of the most influential scientists of the 20th century. His groundbreaking contributions to physics revolutionized our understanding of the universe and laid the foundation for modern physics. Born in Ulm, in the Kingdom of Württemberg in the German Empire, Einstein showed an early interest in mathematics and science.
  • Einstein's most famous work is his theory of relativity, which he developed in the early 20th century. In 1905, he published his paper on the special theory of relativity, introducing the famous equation which expresses the equivalence of mass and energy. This theory fundamentally changed the way scientists understand space, time, and gravity, and it has had profound implications for physics, cosmology, and philosophy.
  • In 1915, Einstein completed his general theory of relativity, which provided a new understanding of gravity as the curvature of spacetime caused by the presence of mass and energy. This theory predicted phenomena such as gravitational waves, which were finally detected in 2015, confirming Einstein's predictions and earning him posthumous acclaim.
  • Einstein also made significant contributions to quantum mechanics, particularly with his work on the photoelectric effect, for which he received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921. His insights into the nature of light and matter helped lay the groundwork for the development of quantum theory.
